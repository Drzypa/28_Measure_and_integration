quasi-invariant


Definition 1.

Let (E,ℬ) be a measurable space, and T:E→E be a measurable map. A measure μ on (E,ℬ) is said to be quasi-invariant under T if μ∘T-1 is absolutely continuous with respect to μ. That is, for all A∈ℬ with μ⁢(A)=0, we also have μ⁢(T-1⁢(A))=0. We also say that T leaves μ quasi-invariant.

As a example, let E=ℝ with ℬ the Borel σ-algebra (http://planetmath.org/BorelSigmaAlgebra), and μ be Lebesgue measure. If T⁢(x)=x+5, then μ is quasi-invariant under T. If S⁢(x)=0, then μ is not quasi-invariant under S. (We have μ⁢({0})=0, but μ⁢(T-1⁢({0}))=μ⁢(ℝ)=∞).

To give another example, take E to be the nonnegative integers and declare every subset of E to be a measurable set. Fix λ>0. Let μ⁢({n})=λnn! and extend μ to all subsets by additivity. Let T be the shift function: n→n+1. Then μ is quasi-invariant under T and not invariant (http://planetmath.org/HaarMeasure).
