Lebesgue density theorem


Let μ be the Lebesgue measure on ℝn, and for a measurable set A⊂ℝn define the density of A in ϵ-neighborhood of x∈ℝn by

dϵ⁢(x)=μ⁢(A∩Bϵ⁢(x))μ⁢(Bϵ⁢(x))

where Bϵ⁢(x) denotes the ball of radius ϵ centered at x.

The Lebesgue density theorem asserts that for almost every point of A the density

d⁢(x)=limϵ→0⁡dϵ⁢(x)

exists and is equal to 1.

In other words, for every measurable set A the density of A is 0 or 1 almost everywhere. However, it is a curious fact that if μ⁢(A)>0 and μ⁢(ℝn∖A)>0, then there are always points of ℝn where the density is neither 0 nor 1 [1, Lemma 4].
