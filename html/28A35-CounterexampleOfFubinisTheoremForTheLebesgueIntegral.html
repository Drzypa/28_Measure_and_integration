counter-example of Fubini’s theorem for the Lebesgue integral


The following observation demonstrates the necessity of the integrability assumption in Fubini’s theorem. Let

Q={(x,y)∈ℝ2:x≥0,y≥0}

denote the upper, right quadrant. Let R⊂Q be the region in the quadrant bounded by the lines y=x,y=x-1, and let let S⊂Q be a similar region, but this time bounded by the lines y=x-1,y=x-2. Let

f=χS-χR,

where χ denotes a characteristic function.

Observe that the Lebesgue measure of R and of S is infinite. Hence, f is not a Lebesgue-integrable function. However for every x≥0 the function

g⁢(x)=∫0∞f⁢(x,y)⁢𝑑y

is integrable. Indeed,

g⁢(x)={-x for ⁢0≤x≤1,x-2 for ⁢1≤x≤2,0 for ⁢x≥2.

Similarly, for y≥0, the function

h⁢(y)=∫0∞f⁢(x,y)⁢𝑑x

is integrable. Indeed,

h⁢(y)=0,y≥0.

Hence, the values of the iterated integrals

∫0∞g⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=-1,
∫0∞h⁢(y)⁢𝑑y=0,

are finite, but do not agree. This does not contradict Fubini’s theorem because the value of the planar Lebesgue integral

∫Qf⁢(x,y)⁢𝑑μ⁢(x,y),

where μ⁢(x,y) is the planar Lebesgue measure, is not defined.
