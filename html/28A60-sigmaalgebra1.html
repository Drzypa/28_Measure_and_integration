σ-algebra


Introduction

When defining a measure for a set E we usually cannot hope to make every subset of E measurable. Instead we must usually restrict our attention to a specific collection of subsets of E, requiring that this collection be closed under operations that we would expect to preserve measurability. A σ-algebra is such a collection.

Definition

Given a set E, a σ-algebra in E is a collection ℱ of subsets of E such that:

  • •

    ∅∈ℱ.

  • •

    Any union of countably many elements of ℱ is an element of ℱ.

  • •

    The complement of any element of ℱ in E is an element of ℱ.

Notes

It follows from the definition that any σ-algebra ℱ in E also satisfies the properties:

  • •

    E∈ℱ.

  • •

    Any intersection of countably many elements of ℱ is an element of ℱ.

Note that a σ-algebra is a field of sets that is closed under countable unions and countable intersections (rather than just finite unions and finite intersections).

Given any collection C of subsets of E, the σ-algebra σ⁢(C) generated by C is defined to be the smallest σ-algebra in E such that C⊆σ⁢(C). This is well-defined, as the intersection of any non-empty collection of σ-algebras in E is also a σ-algebra in E.

Examples

For any set E, the power set 𝒫⁢(E) is a σ-algebra in E, as is the set {∅,E}.

A more interesting example is the Borel σ-algebra (http://planetmath.org/BorelSigmaAlgebra) in ℝ, which is the σ-algebra generated by the open subsets of ℝ, or, equivalently, the σ-algebra generated by the compact subsets of ℝ.
